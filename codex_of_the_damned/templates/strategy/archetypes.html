{{ _("Archetypes") }}

{% trans trimmed %} V:tES has been played for years, and this has given birth to a number of efficient decks archetypes. Given the multiplayer nature of the game, many archetypes are valid choices at the highest level, as the efficiency of a deck depends a lot on its table placement and on the negociations skills of the player. Even on the modern tournament scene, dozens of different archetypes are usually played in a single event. {% endtrans %}

{% trans trimmed %} As such, having a basic understanding of the most common archetypes and their strong and weak points is a very important skill for a player. Knowing the inner workings of the archetypes on the table is the only way to fathom the table balance and detect the opportunities as they arise during the game. {% endtrans %}

{% trans trimmed %} The archetypes are usually divided into broader categories, as presented here. {% endtrans %}

{{ _("Bleed") }}

{% trans trimmed %} Bleed decks are focused on prey aggression and use bleed actions to inflict heavy pool damages. {{ dom }} is without question the most damage-heavy bleed discipline, plus it does come with bounce cards. {{ pre }} and {{ dem }} are good second choices. {% endtrans %}

{{ _("Stealth & Bleed and Powerbleed") }}

{% trans trimmed %} There are two ways to get bleeds through: using stealth is the most obvious one. {{ obf }} is the best stealth discipline, but it does not often come along with {{ dom }}. {{ obt }} and {{ pro }} come second. {% endtrans %}

{% trans trimmed seduction=card("Seduction"), elder_impersonation=card("Elder Impersonation"), call_of_the_hungry_dead=card("Call of the Hungry Dead"), daring_the_dawn=card("Daring the Dawn"), mind_numb=card("Mind Numb"), pentex_subversion=card("Pentex™ Subversion"), misdirection=card("Misdirection"), anarch_troublemaker=card("Anarch Troublemaker") %} The other way to get bleeds through is to make sure the prey has no minion available to block the action. Along its strong bleed capabilities, {{ dom }} offers the excellent {{ seduction }}. {{ OBF }} {{ elder_impersonation }}, {{ NEC }} {{ call_of_the_hungry_dead }}, {{ for }} {{ daring_the_dawn }} and {{ pre }} {{ mind_numb }} are also effective tools. Combining these options with the few Master cards that can prevent block or lock minions — {{ pentex_subversion }}, {{ misdirection }} and {{ anarch_troublemaker }} — makes stealth and intercept irrelevant. {% endtrans %}

{{ _("Highlights") }}

{% trans trimmed %} Bleed decks have the highest prey damage. They tend to oust their first prey quite often, but it also attracts the table attention on them and one oust is not enough for a game win. They are usually weak in combat. They tend to rely on « combat ends » effects, but some flavours can have aggravated damage or torpor strikes as a threat. {% endtrans %}

{{ _("Stealth rotation") }}

{% trans trimmed %} Stealth & Bleed archetypes have a strong stealth module, but it can jam the hand. If the prey assumes she will not have the means to block, she should decide not to attempt to block at all, and try to jam her predator hand on the stealth module. Well constructed Stealth & Bleed decks include some rotation tools to help with that. {% endtrans %}

{% trans trimmed %} As they are focused on their prey, bleed decks have a weak defence against their predator and do not try to intercept actions. They usually use {{ dom }} or {{ AUS }} for bounces or strong bloat modules to survive. {% endtrans %}

{{ _("Vote") }}

{% trans trimmed %} Vote decks usually have a strong pool damage module to deliver to their prey. The difficulty to gather enough votes to get the referendums through is balanced by the constant +1 stealth bonus of vote actions. There are basically two ways of building a vote deck: either breed and find a way to give votes to all your vampires, or use older vampires with titles themselves. {% endtrans %}

{{ _("Highlights") }}

{% trans trimmed consanguineous_boon=card("Consanguineous Boon"), parity_shift=card("Parity Shift"), ancient_inluence=card("Ancient Influence"), political_stranglehold=card("Political Stranglehold") %} Votes decks deliver high pool damage once they get the vote lock. They also have a good bloat, using {{ consanguineous_boon }}, {{ parity_shift }}, {{ ancient_inluence }} or {{ political_stranglehold }} depending on the build. Depending on the exact build, stealth vary, but they are not easily blocked. Builds that do not have high stealth levels rely on a swarm of minions that make it hard to block them all. Finally, vote decks have a very weak combat module and — especially the swarm builds — a weak bleed defence, relying exclusively on bounces, if at all. {% endtrans %}

{{ _("Table Talk") }}

{% trans trimmed table_talk=link("/strategy/table-talk") %} Votes can go either ways and can be targeted effortlessly across the table. This gives unique negotiation options to vote decks, that can be used to keep a low profile and a lot of friends throughout the game. {{ table_talk }} is always important in the game, but even more so for vote archetypes. {% endtrans %}

{{ _("Wall") }}

{% trans trimmed eyes_of_argus=card("Eyes of Argus"), eternal_vigilance=card("Eternal Vigilance"), under_siege=card("Under Siege"), second_tradition_domain=card("Second Tradition: Domain"), no_secrets_from_the_magaji=card("No Secrets From the Magaji") %} Wall decks maximise their defence, trying to block their prey and predator actions alike while slowly building up bloat and pool damages. The ability to intercept and wake are tantamount in these decks, so {{ aus }} and {{ ani }} are the most common options here. {{ aus }} gives more options and a higher intercept all in all, with the excellent {{ eyes_of_argus }} atop. It is also the only discipline besides {{ dom }} that provides bleed bounce reaction cards. {{ ani }} also has good intercept cards though, and better combat options than {{ aus }}: it can be used with no additional equipment or discipline. Sabbat, Camarilla and Laibon titled vampires each have access to excellent intercept cards: {{ eternal_vigilance }}, {{ under_siege }}, {{ second_tradition_domain }} and {{ no_secrets_from_the_magaji }}. {% endtrans %}

{{ _("Flavours") }}

  • {% trans trimmed weenie_aus=link("/archetypes/runner-ups/weenie-aus") %} {{ AUS }} is the best intercept discipline, and the {{ weenie_aus }} wall is an obvious idea. {% endtrans %}
  • {% trans trimmed tzimisce_wall=link("/archetypes/fear-factor", "Tzimisce Wall") %} The {{ tzimisce_wall }} is also a very classic archetype, as the combination of {{ ani }}{{ vic }} offers even better intercept capabilities. {% endtrans %}
  • {% trans trimmed goratrix=card("Goratrix"), goratrix_high_tower=link("/archetypes/goratrix-high-tower") %} Tremere {{ tremere }} in general, but {{ goratrix }} in particular offer excellent wall capabilities in the {{ goratrix_high_tower }} archetype. {% endtrans %}
  • {% trans trimmed cats=link("/archetypes/cats") %} The {{ cats }} archetype uses Ahrimanes {{ ahrimane }} and their {{SPI}}{{ANI}} disciplines combination. {% endtrans %}
  • {% trans trimmed saulot=card("Saulot, The Wanderer", "Saulot"), saulot_wall=link("/archetypes/saulot-wall") %} {{ saulot }} has a nice discipline combination with {{AUS}}{{THA}}{{FOR}} and +1 bleed. The {{ saulot_wall }} archetype is built around him. {% endtrans %}

{{ _("Highlights") }}

{% trans trimmed smiling_jack_the_anarch=card("Smiling Jack, the Anarch"), the_rack=card("The Rack"), powerbase_montreal=card("Powerbase: Montreal"), powerbase_barranquilla=card("Powerbase: Barranquilla"), anarch_revolt=card("Anarch Revolt") %} Wall decks obviously have an exceptional defence. Moreover, having high block capabilities let them play fragile master cards that need protection to be efficient like {{ smiling_jack_the_anarch }}, {{ the_rack }}, {{ powerbase_montreal }}, {{ powerbase_barranquilla }} (Sabbat) and {{ anarch_revolt }} (Anarchs), helping both with bloat and offence. Still, wall decks tend to have a weak offence, dealing only a moderate amount of damage to their prey. They're very good at exhausting other decks around the table though, and will eventually win a game if no other player can get enough Victory Points first. {% endtrans %}

{{ _("Cross block") }}

{% trans trimmed eagles_sight=card("Eagle's Sight"), falcons_eye=card("Falcon's Eye") %} {{ eagles_sight }} and {{ falcons_eye }} are the only minion cards in the game that let a vampire block any action, even if it is directed upon another Methusalah or undertaken cross table. {% endtrans %}

{% trans trimmed %} This ability to cross block is an excellent asset in wall decks with {{ AUS }} or {{ SPI }} as it enhances table control and allows for example to intercept a dangerous vote from the grand-predator or a bleed from the prey that could be lethal to the grand-prey. Wall decks that do not use these will often include some rush capabilities instead. {% endtrans %}

{{ _("Toolbox") }}

{% trans trimmed %} Toolbox decks balance their offence and defence capabilities. They have good intercept, a decent combat module and efficient pool damage options to use on their prey, sometimes even some votes they can try to pass or a bit of stealth. The key to this archetype is to use versatile cards offering multiple play options. {% endtrans %}

  • {% trans trimmed war_chantry=link("/archetypes/war-chantry") %} Tremere {{ tremere }} have a good disciplines combination for a toolbox too, as proves the {{ war_chantry }} archetype. {% endtrans %}

{{ _("Highlights") }}

{% trans trimmed %} Versatility is obviously the best asset of a toolbox deck. It can handle and exploit any table configuration. To make the most out of toolbox decks, one has to display excellent table awareness and negotiation abilities. For the toolbox to have a proper effect around the table and display all its efficiency, one needs to play their hand, whatever is in it. The deck chooses the strategy as much as the Methusalah. The player still chooses on which target to use its cards, but the cards in hand demand to be played for the deck to shine. {% endtrans %}

{{ _("The Heart of Cheating") }}

{% trans trimmed heart_of_nizchetus=card("Heart of Nizchetus") %} The {{ heart_of_nizchetus }} is often included in toolbox builds, as it gives more control to the Methusalah over what his play can be. Its effect makes such a difference in efficiency it has been nicknamed « The Heart of Cheating ». {% endtrans %}

{% trans trimmed %} Finally, toolbox decks have strong defence, although not as strong as wall decks. They tend to have good intercept options and/or rush capabilities to handle their predator. Having much less offensive options than a vote or bleed deck, they cannot rely on pure speed to best their predator: they have to deter its actions actively using blocks, bounces or combats depending on the build. {% endtrans %}

{{ _("Rush") }}

{% trans trimmed fame=card("Fame"), dragonbound=card("Dragonbound"), tension_in_the_ranks=card("Tension in the Ranks") %} Rush decks use combat as their main weapon. Many disciplines have good combat options and they are also good combat cards with no discipline. Getting vampires down around the table is fun, but does not help so much to get the prey's pool down. To this effect, a handful of cards can help, namely {{ fame }}, {{ dragonbound }} and {{ tension_in_the_ranks }}. {% endtrans %}

{{ _("Flavours") }}

  • {% trans trimmed tupdogs=link("/archetypes/tupdogs-nephandus"), tupdog=card("Tupdog") %} The {{ tupdogs }} archetype is a classic, using a dozen {{ tupdog }} along a Tremere Antitribu. {% endtrans %}
  • {% trans trimmed war_ghouls=link("/archetypes/bibliodeque/war-ghouls"), shambling_hordes=link("/archetypes/runner-ups/shambling-hordes") %} Allies with a rush ability are also good, like in the {{ war_ghouls }} and {{ shambling_hordes }} archetypes. {% endtrans %}
  • {% trans trimmed guruhi_rush=link("/archetypes/guruhi-rush"), nananimalism=link("/archetypes/nananimalism") %} Guruhi {{ guruhi }} provide a strong rush build too with their {{POT}}{{ANI}} disciplines combination, like in the {{ guruhi_rush }} and {{ nananimalism }} archetypes. {% endtrans %}

{{ _("Highlights") }}

{% trans trimmed %} The ability to take down any minion provide these decks with a strong defence against their predator. Depending on the predator archetype, going against its minions first is a common strategy for a rush deck: it may take some combats to get them down, but they can disable their predator very fast. On the other hand, they have a weak offence and have trouble finding a way to deliver pool damage to their prey. This is the core issue of the archetype. {% endtrans %}

{{ _("Finnish Politics") }}

{% trans trimmed %} If they do not take down their prey very fast, rush decks have a unique control over the table balance as they can take down the minions of any Methusalah. Proper table control is essential to the succes of such builds, but it also opens a unique strategy for negociation: using the threat of combat to get things out of your neighbours. {% endtrans %}

{% trans trimmed %} The finnish players have perfect this negociation strategy to such an extent, it is now known as "Finnish Politics" among seasoned players. {% endtrans %}

{{ _("Highlights") }}

{% trans trimmed %} Special decks tend to use complicated card combinations to a devastative effect. Their strength is in the absolute advantage their combination provides if all goes well. Their weakness is the frailty of such a combination. Blocking the right action or countering the right Master can often stop them dead in their tracks. Moreover, known combinations tend to attract the attention of all players, often leading the Methusalah to lose all its allies very early in the game. {% endtrans %}
